# How Solo and Small Firms Scale with AI: More Cases, Same Headcount

There's a ceiling every small firm hits. You're good, the work comes in, and then it stops — not because demand dried up, but because you ran out of you. Every new matter needs your attention, every document needs your drafting, and there are only so many billable hours in a week. The usual fix is to hire, but hiring is slow, expensive, and risky for a firm of two or three. So you cap your growth instead, and tell yourself you're "at capacity."

You're not at capacity. You're at the capacity of your *current workflow*. This page is about raising that ceiling with AI — handling more cases with the same headcount by removing the repetitive drafting and lookup work that eats your day. We don't teach AI tools — we show you how to grow your business with AI. That maps to the **T — Transform** stage of the [GROWT Method](/en/growt-method): the point where AI stops being a productivity tweak and becomes the reason your firm can grow without growing its overhead.

First, the rule that protects you.

## The discipline that comes first

Confidential and privileged material never goes into a public AI tool. Not draft pleadings tied to a real matter, not client facts, not anything a client told you in confidence. A paste into a free chatbot can be a breach of confidentiality and may waive privilege.

The workflows below are built so the AI works with *templates, structure, and anonymized patterns* — your precedents, your standard clauses, your process — not live client data. When AI genuinely needs to touch a real matter, that belongs in an enterprise tool with a data processing agreement, zero data retention, and your firm's controls. And one line you do not cross: AI drafts; a qualified lawyer reviews and is responsible. The model is a tireless junior, not a decision-maker. Treat every output as a first draft you sign off on.

## Why solo and small firms struggle to scale

Three things keep the ceiling low.

**Everything is bespoke — even when it isn't.** A huge share of legal work is variations on a theme: the same engagement letter with different names, the same type of clause adapted to a new deal, the same advice email reframed for a similar situation. It *feels* bespoke because each one needs care, but most of the effort is reconstruction, not judgment.

**Your knowledge lives in your head.** When the answer to "how do we handle this?" is only in the senior partner's memory, the firm can't scale past that partner's available hours. Nothing is documented, so nothing can be delegated.

**Delegation is risky without supervision capacity.** You'd love to hand work down, but checking a junior's work takes time too — and for a firm of two, there often isn't a junior. So the partner does everything, and the everything is the ceiling.

AI attacks all three. It turns bespoke-feeling work into fast first drafts, it captures firm knowledge into reusable form, and it gives you a "junior" whose drafts you check instead of build.

## The GROWT Method approach

**G — Gap.** Track where your hours actually go for two weeks. Most lawyers are stunned by how much time goes into drafting that's really re-drafting, and into looking up things the firm already knows. That re-work is your gap — and it's exactly what AI is good at compressing.

**R — Roadmap.** Pick the one or two document types or tasks that consume the most repetitive time and would benefit most from a system. Don't try to automate everything at once. A roadmap that says "standardize our three most common letter types this quarter" is real; "use AI everywhere" is not.

**O — Operationalize.** Build the reusable assets: prompt-driven templates for your common documents, a structured knowledge base of your firm's standard answers, a clause library the model can draft from. This is the unglamorous engine room — and it's where scaling actually happens.

**W — Win.** Capture the gains. The drafting that took two hours takes thirty minutes of generating-and-checking. The lookup that interrupted your afternoon takes one query. You take on the extra matter you'd have declined, because the marginal hour it costs you just dropped.

**T — Transform.** Now the firm runs on systems rather than on one person's memory and stamina. You can take more work, onboard help faster, and step away without everything stopping. That's the transformation: the same people, a meaningfully higher ceiling.

## Workflows you can use this week

Copy-paste starting points. Adapt the brackets, work from templates and anonymized material, and review every output as the responsible lawyer.

### 1. First-draft a standard document from your own template

The fastest win is letting AI assemble a first draft of a document you produce constantly — from *your* template, with *your* clauses, with placeholders instead of real client data.

> "Here is our firm's standard [letter of engagement] template, with placeholders in [BRACKETS]: [paste template]. Here is a structured, anonymized summary of the new matter: [matter type, scope of work, fee basis, key dates — no client name or identifying facts]. Produce a first draft by filling the template appropriately. Where information is missing, insert a clearly marked [TO CONFIRM] flag rather than guessing. Do not add clauses that aren't in the template. List at the end any points a lawyer should check before this is sent."

You get a draft that's 80% there, with the gaps and the "check this" list made explicit. You fill the placeholders with real details *outside* the tool and review the substance.

### 2. Turn your firm's expertise into a knowledge base entry

Scaling means getting answers out of one person's head and into a form the whole firm can use.

> "I'm building an internal knowledge base for my [conveyancing] practice. Based on the following notes from how we handle [a standard leasehold purchase], write a clear internal procedure document: the steps in order, the documents needed at each step, the common pitfalls, and the points where a fee earner must escalate to a partner. Notes: [paste your own anonymized process notes]. Write it for a new fee earner who knows the law but not our process."

Do this for your ten most common procedures and you've built something a new hire — or a delegated task — can actually run on.

### 3. Use AI as a paralegal-style summarizer (on safe material)

When you have a long *non-confidential* document — a public judgment, published guidance, a piece of legislation — AI can compress it fast.

> "Summarize this [published court judgment / piece of guidance] for a busy practitioner. Give me: the key holding in 2–3 sentences, the facts that mattered, the practical takeaway for advising clients in similar situations, and any points that are unclear or fact-specific. Be precise about what the source actually says — do not extrapolate beyond it or invent citations."

This is genuinely useful for keeping current, and it stays safe because the input is public. The "do not invent citations" instruction is not optional — AI fabricates case references with total confidence, and a fabricated citation in front of a court is a career risk. Verify every reference against the primary source, every time.

### 4. Standardize and improve a clause you reuse

> "Here is a [confidentiality clause] we reuse across matters: [paste clause]. Rewrite it for clarity and plain English without changing its legal effect. Then flag any ambiguity in the original that could be read against us, and suggest a tighter alternative. Mark anything where the change might alter the legal meaning so I can review it carefully."

You end up with a cleaner precedent and a list of issues a lawyer should decide on — improving your library, not just your day.

## What to expect

Scaling is a build, and the payoff compounds:

- **Weeks 1–2:** Track your hours, find the gap, and set up your first AI-assisted document template. Most firms see a same-week win on whichever document they produce most.
- **Weeks 3–6:** Convert your three most common documents to prompt-driven first drafts and write your first five knowledge-base procedures. Drafting time on those documents typically drops by half once the template and prompt are dialed in.
- **Months 2–3:** The knowledge base starts paying off — questions that used to interrupt a partner get answered from the documented process. Delegation becomes realistic because there's something to delegate *to*.
- **Months 3–6:** The ceiling is visibly higher. A two-person firm we worked with took on roughly 20% more matters across a quarter without adding staff — the reclaimed drafting and lookup time absorbed the load, and every output still passed through a lawyer's review.

The point isn't to bill less per matter. It's to fit more of the right matters into the same week, safely.

## FAQ

**Will AI replace my paralegal or junior staff?**
No — it changes what they do. The repetitive drafting and summarizing AI handles frees your people for the work that needs a human: judgment, client relationships, supervision. Most small firms use AI to *avoid premature hiring*, then hire for higher-value roles when growth justifies it.

**Can I trust AI with legal drafting?**
You can trust it to produce a first draft from your own templates. You cannot trust it to be correct, current, or jurisdiction-appropriate without your review. Treat it like a fast, tireless junior whose work you always check.

**What's the single biggest risk?**
Two: confidentiality (never paste privileged or client-identifying material into public tools) and fabricated citations (AI invents case references convincingly — verify every one against the primary source). Build both checks into your process from day one.

**Do I need a special legal AI product?**
Not to start. A standard AI assistant plus disciplined data handling covers the workflows above. Purpose-built legal tools with DPAs and zero retention make sense when you want AI to work with real matter data — that's a later, deliberate step.

**How do I keep quality consistent if AI does the drafting?**
By templating. When the draft always starts from your approved template and clause library, the output is consistent by construction. The model fills and adapts; your standards are baked into what it starts from.

**Where do I start if I'm short on time?**
Pick the one document you produce most and build a template-plus-prompt for it this week. One win proves the model and funds the time for the next.
